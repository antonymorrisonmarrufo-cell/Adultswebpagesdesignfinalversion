## Description

You have the right to make your own decisions. If there are concerns about whether you are able to make a specific decision, the law sets out clear rules to protect you.

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 starts from the principle that every adult has the right to make their own decisions. You must be assumed to have capacity unless it is established otherwise. Making a decision that others disagree with does not mean you lack capacity.

This is a legal protection to make sure that any restrictions on a person's freedom are lawful, necessary, and in their best interests.

## Key Points

- Assume capacity. Every adult is assumed to be able to make their own decisions unless it is proved otherwise.
- Support decision-making. Before deciding that someone lacks capacity, all practical steps must be taken to help them make the decision themselves.
- Unwise decisions are allowed. A person is not to be treated as lacking capacity just because they make a decision that others consider unwise.
- Best interests. If a decision must be made on behalf of someone who lacks capacity, it must be made in their best interests.
- Least restrictive option. Any action taken on behalf of someone who lacks capacity should be the least restrictive of their rights and freedom.
- Understand the information relevant to the decision
- Retain that information long enough to make the decision
