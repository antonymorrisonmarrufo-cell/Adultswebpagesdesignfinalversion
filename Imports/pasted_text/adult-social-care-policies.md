## PAGE 2: CHARGING AND FINANCIAL POLICIES (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Our policies > Charging and financial policies

**H1:** Charging and financial policies

**Standfirst:** The policies that set out how the council calculates what people pay towards the cost of their adult social care.

### How charging works

Adult social care is means-tested. If you are assessed as having eligible care and support needs, the council will carry out a financial assessment to work out whether you need to contribute towards the cost of your care. The amount you pay depends on your income, savings, assets, and the type of care you receive.

The rules for how contributions are calculated are set out in the policies below. These policies follow the national framework set by the Care Act 2014 and the government's charging guidance.

For a plain English explanation of how charges work, see our paying for your care pages.

### Fairer Contributions Policy 2025/26

This policy sets out how the council calculates contributions for non-residential care and support — that is, care you receive at home or in the community, rather than in a care home.

It covers the Minimum Income Guarantee (MIG), which ensures that you keep a minimum level of income after paying towards your care. It also explains how disability-related expenditure is taken into account.

**Download:** Fairer Contributions Policy 2025/26 (PDF)

### Residential Care Charging Policy

This policy sets out how the council calculates contributions for residential care — that is, care provided in a care home.

It covers how the council assesses your income and capital, the treatment of property, and the rules around third-party top-up payments.

**Download:** Residential Care Charging Policy (PDF)

*Note: This policy is dated 2022. Confirm with the finance team whether an updated version is available.*

### Deferred Payment Agreements

A deferred payment agreement allows people who own their home to delay paying some of their care home costs, instead of selling the property immediately. The council places a legal charge against the property and recovers the deferred amount later.

Full information about deferred payment agreements, including eligibility, how to apply, interest charges, and set-up costs, is available on our dedicated page.

**Link:** Deferred payment agreements (under Paying for your care)

### Direct Payment Scheme

Direct payments give people more choice and control over how their care and support is arranged. This document explains how the direct payment scheme works, including eligibility, what you can spend it on, and your responsibilities.

**Download:** Direct Payment Scheme information (PDF)

### Safeguarding Adults Board

The Care Act 2014 requires every local authority to set up a Safeguarding Adults Board (SAB). The board brings together the council, the NHS, the police, and other organisations to protect adults who have care and support needs and may be at risk.

The Waltham Forest SAB works to ensure that partner organisations are working together effectively to prevent abuse and neglect and to respond when concerns are raised.

For more information, contact: strategicpartnerships@walthamforest.gov.uk

## PAGE 4: MARKET SUSTAINABILITY AND COMMISSIONING (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Our policies > Market sustainability and commissioning

**H1:** Market sustainability and commissioning

**Standfirst:** Information for care providers, commissioners, and the public about how the council ensures a sustainable and fair adult social care market.

### Fair Cost of Care

In 2022, the government required local authorities to carry out a fair cost of care exercise to understand the actual cost of providing care in their area. The purpose is to ensure that the prices paid to care providers are sustainable and fair.

The council completed this exercise following guidance from the Department of Health and Social Care. The reports below set out the findings.

**Download:** Cost of Care Report — Residential and Nursing Care for ages 65+ (Annex B, PDF)

**Download:** Cost of Care Report — Domiciliary Care for ages 18+ (Annex B, PDF)

### Market Sustainability Plan

Local authorities are also required to publish a Market Sustainability Plan setting out how they intend to move towards paying a fair cost of care. The plan covers the current sustainability of local care markets, the impact of future changes, and the council's approach to addressing sustainability issues.

**Download:** Market Sustainability Plan (PDF)

### Market Position Statements

The council publishes Market Position Statements (MPS) to give care providers and commissioners a clear picture of the local market. These cover demand, supply, quality, spending, and commissioning intentions across different service areas.

**Link:** View Market Position Statements

### What is a Market Position Statement?

Under Section 5 of the Care Act 2014, the council has a duty to promote the efficient and effective operation of the adult social care market. This includes ensuring that people have a variety of providers to choose from and that providers offer a variety of high-quality services.

Market Position Statements are one of the ways the council meets this duty. They provide transparency about what the council needs, what is currently available, and where there are gaps.

### For providers

If you are a care provider and want to know more about commissioning opportunities in Waltham Forest, or if you have questions about the market information published here, contact the Adult Social Care Commissioning team.

## PAGE 5: PRIVACY AND DATA (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Our policies > Privacy and data

**H1:** Privacy and data

**Standfirst:** How the council collects, uses, and shares your personal information when providing adult social care services.

### Adult Social Care Privacy Notice

When providing care and support services, the council must collect and use your personal information. The Adult Social Care Privacy Notice explains what information we collect, why we need it, who we may share it with, and your rights.

The privacy notice covers information collected about people who receive or apply for adult social care services, including those with physical disabilities, learning disabilities, mental health conditions, and their carers.

**Link:** Read the Adult Social Care Privacy Notice

### Client Level Data — NHS England

Since 2023, the council is required by the Secretary of State for Health to submit detailed client-level adult social care data to NHS England. This is a national requirement that applies to all councils in England.

The data includes information about you such as your date of birth, postcode, and details about your care and support. The purpose is to help the NHS and local authorities understand population needs and plan services more effectively.

**Link:** Read the Client Level Data Privacy Notice

### Your rights

Under data protection law, you have the right to:

- Ask what personal information the council holds about you
- Ask for incorrect information to be corrected
- Ask for your information to be deleted in certain circumstances
- Object to how your information is being used
- Ask for your information to be provided in a portable format

If you have questions about how your data is used in adult social care, contact: mashrequests@walthamforest.gov.uk

For more information about data protection at Waltham Forest, see the council's main privacy policy page.

### Current consultations

**Adult Social Care Charging Policy Consultation**

The council is consulting on a proposal to change the way it calculates how much people pay towards their care and support at home and in the community.

The consultation relates to the Minimum Income Guarantee (MIG) top-up — an additional amount above the statutory minimum that residents are currently allowed to keep before being asked to contribute towards care costs. The council is proposing to reduce this top-up over two years, from 25% to 10% and then to 0%, bringing Waltham Forest in line with many other councils.

This change could affect approximately 2,500 residents who receive community care and support. Of those, around 1,400 currently pay nothing towards their care. If the top-up is reduced, around 350 of those residents may begin contributing a small amount.
