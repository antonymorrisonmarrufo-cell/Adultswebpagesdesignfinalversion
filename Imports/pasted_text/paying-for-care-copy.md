H1
Paying for your care
STANDFIRST
Find out how care costs work, what you might need to pay, and where to get financial help and advice.

How the process works
BODY COPY
If you are assessed as needing care and support from the council, we may carry out a financial assessment to work out whether you need to pay towards the cost. This is how it works:

1.	We assess your care needs. A care needs assessment looks at the help you need and whether you have eligible care and support needs. This is always free.
2.	We work out the cost of your support. If you qualify, we work out how much it will cost to meet your eligible needs.
3.	We assess your finances. We ask about your income, spending, savings and any other assets so we can work out what you can afford to pay.
4.	We tell you the outcome. We explain whether you need to pay all, some or none of the cost, and what happens next.
5.	We arrange payment. If charges apply, we explain how to pay and when you will receive your first invoice.

A care needs assessment is always free. You do not need to pay anything to find out whether you are eligible for support.

Key things to know
•	Some people pay the full cost of their care, some pay a contribution, and some pay nothing. It depends on your individual financial circumstances.
•	If you have savings and assets over £23,250, you will usually pay your own care costs. This is called self-funding.
•	If you own your home and need to move into a care home, a deferred payment agreement may help you avoid selling your home straight away.
•	You may be entitled to benefits you are not currently claiming. These can reduce or remove your contribution towards care costs.
•	If you are already receiving care, you can pay your adult social care bill online.

H1
How care costs are worked out
STANDFIRST
If you receive care or support arranged by the council, you may need to pay all or part of the cost. How much you pay depends on your income, savings, and personal circumstances.

How we work out what you pay
If you are assessed as having eligible care and support needs, we use your assessment to understand your needs and work out the cost of your care and support plan. We then carry out a financial assessment to work out your contribution. This is based on your individual financial circumstances.

When charges start
Charges start from the date your services begin. However, they do not come into effect until your financial assessment has been completed. You will not receive an invoice until we have finished assessing your finances and told you the outcome.

What you might need to pay
There are three possible outcomes from a financial assessment:

•	You pay nothing. If your income is below a protected minimum level, you will not be asked to contribute towards your care costs.
•	You pay a contribution. If you have some income above the protected minimum, you may be asked to pay a weekly amount towards your care. The exact amount depends on your circumstances.
•	You pay the full cost. If you have savings and assets over £23,250, you will usually be expected to pay the full cost of your care. This is called self-funding.

What happens after your financial assessment
After your financial assessment, we will:

•	Calculate what you can afford to pay
•	Explain clearly how your contribution has been worked out
•	Tell you how to pay
•	Explain what to do if your financial circumstances change

You should usually receive your first invoice within seven weeks of the financial assessment visit. If that is not possible, we will write to you to explain why.

If you pay for your own care
If your savings and assets are over £23,250, you will usually pay the full cost of your care. This is often called self-funding.
Even if you are a self-funder, the council can still help you. You have the right to:

•	A care needs assessment, if you want one
•	Information and advice about the types of care available
•	Help finding suitable care providers

You do not have to arrange everything on your own.

If you own your home
If you own your home and are moving into permanent residential care, a deferred payment agreement may allow you to delay some of the cost instead of selling your home straight away. See our page on paying for a care home for more detail.
You should get independent legal and financial advice before making a decision about a deferred payment.

Charging policies
The detailed rules for how contributions are calculated are set out in the council’s charging policies:

•	Fairer Contributions Policy 2025/26 (PDF)
•	Residential Care Charging Policy (PDF)

H1
Financial assessment
STANDFIRST
A financial assessment helps us work out whether you need to pay towards your care and support, and if so, how much.

PRIMARY CTA
▶ Ask about a financial assessment
Links to: Financial Assessment enquiry form on the portal

What a financial assessment is
A financial assessment looks at your income, savings, and assets so we can work out what contribution, if any, you need to make towards the cost of your care. It is separate from your care needs assessment.
Not everyone has to pay. The financial assessment is how we make sure that charges are fair and based on what you can afford.

What information we need from you
Please have details of:

•	Income from benefits, pensions, wages or other sources
•	Regular household and essential spending
•	Savings and investments
•	Property or other assets

What we do with this information
We use the information to:

•	Calculate what you can afford to pay
•	Explain how your contribution has been worked out
•	Check whether you may be entitled to benefits you are not currently claiming
•	Tell you what to do if your finances change in the future

If we find that you are not claiming benefits you are entitled to, we will let the Department for Work and Pensions know so they can help you claim them. This could reduce what you need to pay towards your care.

What if I think my assessment is wrong?
If you think your financial assessment is incorrect, contact us as soon as possible and ask for it to be reviewed. The detailed rules are set out in the council’s Fairer Contributions Policy.

What if I need help?
If you find it difficult to manage your finances, or if you need help understanding the assessment, you can ask a family member, friend or someone else you trust to support you. In some situations, another person can manage financial matters on your behalf.
If you do not have anyone to help and you have difficulty being involved in the process, we can arrange an independent advocate.

H1
Benefits and help with costs
STANDFIRST
You may be entitled to benefits or other financial support that can help with disability-related costs or caring responsibilities. Many people miss out because they do not know what is available.

You may be missing out
Many people who could claim a benefit do not realise they are entitled. Some benefits are paid regardless of how much you earn or have in savings. Others are means-tested, but the rules change regularly, so it is worth checking even if you have been refused in the past.
Claiming the right benefits can also reduce what you are asked to pay towards your care costs. During a financial assessment, we check whether you are receiving everything you are entitled to.

Benefits that may help you
If you have a disability or long-term condition
•	Personal Independence Payment (PIP) – helps with extra costs if you have a long-term health condition or disability and are under State Pension age.
•	Attendance Allowance – for people over State Pension age who need help with personal care or supervision because of a disability or illness. You do not need to have someone caring for you to claim it.

If you are a carer
•	Carer’s Allowance – if you spend at least 35 hours a week caring for someone who receives certain disability benefits.
•	Carer’s Credit – protects your State Pension if you are caring for someone but not earning enough to pay National Insurance.

Other support
•	Council Tax Reduction – you may be able to reduce your council tax bill.
•	Housing Benefit or help with rent through Universal Credit.
•	Pension Credit – if you are over State Pension age and on a low income.
•	Winter Fuel Payment – a yearly payment to help with heating costs.

Attendance Allowance is one of the most under-claimed benefits. You do not need someone caring for you to be eligible. If you are over State Pension age and need help with things like washing, dressing, or getting around safely, it is worth checking whether you qualify.

Where to get help checking your benefits
•	GOV.UK – benefit information and online applications (gov.uk/browse/benefits)
•	Citizens Advice – free, confidential advice on benefits and money
•	Age UK Waltham Forest – advice and support for older residents

H1
Direct payments and personal budgets
STANDFIRST
If you are eligible for care and support, you will receive a personal budget. You can choose how this is managed, including receiving it as a direct payment so you can arrange your own support.

What is a personal budget?
A personal budget is the amount of money needed to meet the cost of your eligible care and support needs. It includes any amount the council pays and any amount you contribute yourself. Everyone whose eligible needs are met by the council receives a personal budget as part of their care and support plan.

How a personal budget can be managed
There are three main ways your personal budget can be used:

•	As a direct payment – the money is paid to you (usually onto a prepaid card) so you can arrange and pay for your own support.
•	As a council-managed account – the council holds the budget and arranges support on your behalf, in line with your wishes.
•	As a third-party managed account – another organisation holds the budget and arranges support on your behalf.

You can also choose a mix of these options. For example, you might take a direct payment for some of your support and ask the council to arrange the rest.

What is a direct payment?
A direct payment gives you the money to arrange your own care and support. Some people use direct payments to employ a personal assistant, hire a care agency of their choice, or pay for activities and services that help them stay independent.
Direct payments can give you more choice and flexibility. You decide who provides your support and when.

Do I have to pay anything myself?
If your financial assessment shows that you need to contribute towards the cost of your care, the council will usually subtract your contribution from your personal budget before paying the direct payment to you. This means you receive the council’s share of the budget, not the full amount.

What can I spend it on?
Your personal budget must be spent on meeting the outcomes agreed in your care and support plan. It cannot be used for anything unrelated to your eligible care needs. You will need to keep records of how the money is spent.

What if I cannot manage it myself?
You do not need to manage a direct payment on your own. A family member, friend, or nominated person can manage it for you. If you lack the capacity to consent to direct payments, an authorised person can request and manage them on your behalf.
The Direct Payment Support Service at Waltham Forest Citizens Advice Bureau offers free help and advice for people using direct payments.

What if I do not want a direct payment?
Direct payments are optional. If you prefer, the council can arrange your care and support directly, or through a managed account. Whatever you choose will be set out in your care and support plan.

H1
Paying for a care home
STANDFIRST
Find out how care home costs work, including self-funding, top-up payments, and deferred payment agreements.

If you pay for your own care home
If your savings and assets are above £23,250, you will usually pay your own care home costs. This is called self-funding.
Even if you are a self-funder, you still have the right to ask the council for a care needs assessment. You can also ask us for information and advice about choosing a care home.

What the council pays
If your savings and assets are below £23,250, the council will usually contribute towards the cost of your care home. You may still need to pay a weekly contribution from your income. This is worked out through a financial assessment.

Top-up payments
If you choose a care home that costs more than the council’s agreed rate, someone will need to pay the difference. This is called a top-up payment.

Who pays the top-up?
The top-up is usually paid by a family member, friend, or other third party directly to the care home. You can only pay the top-up yourself in limited circumstances, including:

•	Where a 12-week property disregard applies
•	Where you have entered into a deferred payment agreement with the council

Important
Top-up arrangements must be affordable for as long as you are in the care home. If the top-up can no longer be paid, it may be necessary to move to a home that accepts the council’s standard rate. Please make sure the arrangement is sustainable before agreeing to it.

Cost increases
Care home fees usually increase each year. You or the person paying the top-up will be responsible for any increase to the top-up amount. The care home must give at least four weeks’ written notice of any change, and increases can only take effect on the anniversary of the placement.

Deferred payment agreements
If you own your home and are moving into permanent residential care, a deferred payment agreement may allow you to delay paying some of the cost. The council pays the care home on your behalf and places a legal charge against your property. The deferred amount is repaid later, usually when the property is sold or from your estate.

Who is eligible?
Deferred payment agreements are generally available to people whose non-property assets (savings, investments and other capital excluding the value of the home) are below £23,250.

Get advice first
A deferred payment agreement is a significant financial decision. You should get independent legal and financial advice before agreeing to one. The council can provide information, but we cannot give you personal financial advice.

H1
Get financial advice
STANDFIRST
Before making big decisions about paying for care, it is usually best to get impartial financial advice from a qualified professional.

Why advice matters
Planning for care costs can involve decisions about savings, pensions, property, insurance, and how your affairs would be managed if you could no longer manage them yourself. An independent financial adviser can help you understand your options and make sure you get the best outcome for your situation.
The council can give you general information, but we cannot give you personal financial advice. An independent adviser will look at your specific circumstances and recommend what is best for you.

Finding an adviser
When choosing a financial adviser, check that they are authorised by the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA). For care-related advice, look for an adviser who specialises in later-life planning.

•	FCA Register – check whether an adviser is authorised (register.fca.org.uk)
•	Society of Later Life Advisers (SOLLA) – find an adviser who specialises in financial planning for older people (societyoflaterlifeadvisers.co.uk)
•	MoneyHelper – free, impartial guidance on choosing a financial adviser (moneyhelper.org.uk)

Free advice and support
If you need free advice about benefits, debt, or managing your money:

•	Citizens Advice Waltham Forest – free, confidential advice on benefits, debt, and money
•	Age UK Waltham Forest – advice and support for older residents
•	MoneyHelper – help with debt, budgeting, and financial planning

If you are worried about debt
Getting into debt is nothing to be ashamed of, and the sooner you get help, the easier it is to manage. If you are struggling to pay bills or keep up with payments, contact Citizens Advice or MoneyHelper for free, confidential support.
Do not ignore the problem. Many organisations will work with you to find a solution.

Planning ahead
Even if you do not need care now, it is worth thinking about:

•	Whether your savings and pension will support you in later life
•	Whether you could afford to pay for care if you needed it
•	Whether your finances and affairs would be properly looked after if you were no longer able to manage them yourself
•	Whether you have a valid will
