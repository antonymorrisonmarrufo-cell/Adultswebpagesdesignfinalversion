# Figma Make Prompt — How to Get Support Section

Create a complete "How to get support" section for a local council adult social care website. This section has one hub landing page and five sub-pages. Use a clean, professional, accessible design consistent with UK local government websites. Use the existing site styling from the Waltham Forest council website. ## IMPORTANT: AVOIDING DUPLICATION WITH "HOW ADULT SOCIAL CARE WORKS"

This section is the ACTION companion to "How adult social care works." The two sections have been designed to work together without overlapping:

- **How adult social care works** = understand the system (what ASC is, who it is for, how eligibility works). That section should contain only 3 pages: the hub, "What is adult social care", and "Who can get help."
- **How to get support** = take action (contact us, check your situation, go through an assessment, get your plan). This is the section below.

If the "How adult social care works" section was built using the previous prompt, Pages 4–6 of that prompt (How to ask for help, What happens during an assessment, Your care and support plan) should be REMOVED from that section. Their content now lives here in "How to get support" instead. This avoids residents finding the same information in two places.

## PAGE 1: HOW TO GET SUPPORT (Hub landing page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How to get support

**H1:** How to get support

**Standfirst:** Find out how to apply for adult social care support, from making first contact through to getting a care and support plan.

### Section: Where to start

If you think you or someone you know might need adult social care, you do not need to wait until things are difficult. Getting in touch early means we can connect you to help before the situation gets worse.

There are two ways to start:

**Check your situation online**
Use our self-assessment tool to answer some questions about your daily life. It takes a few minutes and will tell you whether you might be eligible for support. It will also point you towards information, advice, and local services that could help.

**Button:** Use our self-assessment tool
(Links to the existing self-assessment tool on the portal)

**Contact us directly**
If you prefer to speak to someone, or if you are not sure where to start, call our Adult Social Care team. We can talk through your situation and help you work out what to do next.

Phone: 020 8496 3000
Monday to Friday, 9am to 5pm

**Callout box:** You do not need a referral from a GP or hospital to ask for help. Anyone can contact us — you, a family member, a friend, or a professional.

### Section: The steps from first contact to support

1. Make contact. Phone us or use the online self-assessment tool. We will listen to your situation and advise on the best next step.
2. Check your situation. The self-assessment tool checks whether you might be eligible for support and signposts you to services that could help straight away.
3. Request an assessment. If your situation suggests you may have eligible needs, we will arrange a care needs assessment. This is always free.
4. Have your assessment. A professional from the council will talk with you about your daily life, what you find difficult, and what matters to you. It is a conversation, not a test.
5. Get your plan. If you are eligible, we will work with you to create a care and support plan that sets out what help you will receive and how it will be provided.

## PAGE 2: CHECK YOUR SITUATION ONLINE (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How to get support > Check your situation online

**H1:** Check your situation online

**Standfirst:** Use our self-assessment tool to answer some questions about your situation. It will tell you whether you might be eligible for support and point you towards services that could help.

**Button:** Start the self-assessment tool
(Links to the existing self-assessment tool on the portal)

### What the tool does

The self-assessment tool asks you a series of questions about your daily life — things like how you manage at home, whether you can wash, dress, and eat independently, and how you are coping day to day.

Based on your answers, it will tell you:

- How likely you are to be eligible for council-funded care and support
- Whether you might need to pay towards some or all of your care
- Information, advice, and local services that could help you, even if you do not need a formal assessment

### Who can use it

You can use it for yourself, or you can complete it on behalf of someone else — for example, a parent, partner, or friend you are worried about.

### How long it takes

The tool takes around 10 to 15 minutes to complete. You do not need to create an account to use it, although creating one allows you to save your progress and come back later.

### What happens with your answers

The tool gives you an immediate result based on what you have told us. If the result suggests you may need a formal assessment, you can contact us to arrange one.

Your answers are confidential. The self-assessment does not commit you to anything — it is a way to check your situation and find out what help might be available.

### If you need help using the tool

If you find it difficult to use the online tool, you can contact us by phone instead. We can talk through your situation and help you work out whether an assessment would be the right next step.

Phone: 020 8496 3000
Monday to Friday, 9am to 5pm

**Callout box:** The self-assessment is not a formal care needs assessment. It is a quick way to check your situation and get pointed in the right direction. If you need a full assessment, we will arrange one separately.

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How to get support > Contact us

**Standfirst:** If you think you or someone you know might need adult social care, get in touch. We can talk through your situation and help you work out the best next step.

### Phone

**020 8496 3000**

When you call, we will ask you some basic questions about your situation so we can understand what kind of help you need. If you are calling about someone else, please have their name, date of birth, and address ready if possible.

### Out of hours

If you need urgent social care help outside office hours, call 020 8496 3000 and choose the emergency option.

If someone is in immediate danger, call 999.

### Online

You can use our self-assessment tool at any time, including evenings and weekends. It will check your situation and point you towards services that could help.

**Button:** Use our self-assessment tool
(Links to the existing self-assessment tool on the portal)

### What happens when you contact us

When you get in touch, we will:

- Listen to your situation and understand what kind of help you are looking for
- Give you information and advice about the support available
- Help you decide whether a care needs assessment is the right next step
- If an assessment is needed, explain what happens and arrange a time that suits you

In some cases, we may be able to help straight away by putting you in touch with community services, voluntary organisations, equipment providers, or other support without needing a formal assessment.

### Who can contact us

Anyone can contact us:

- You can call for yourself
- A family member, friend, or carer can call on your behalf
- A GP, nurse, hospital, or other health professional can refer you
- Any other organisation, such as a housing provider, can contact us about you

You do not need a referral or a letter from a doctor. If you think you might need help, that is enough to get in touch.

### If you are a professional

If you are a GP, nurse, social worker, or other professional and you want to refer someone for adult social care support, please phone 020 8496 3000.

## PAGE 4: REQUEST AN ASSESSMENT (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How to get support > Request an assessment

**H1:** Request an assessment

**Standfirst:** A care needs assessment helps us understand your situation and decide what support, if any, would help. It is always free.

### What a care needs assessment is

A care needs assessment is a conversation between you and a professional from the council. It looks at what you can and cannot do, what matters to you, and what kind of support might help you stay as independent as possible.

It is not a test. There are no right or wrong answers. It is about understanding your situation.

### How to request one

To request a care needs assessment, contact the Adult Social Care team:

Phone: 020 8496 3000
Monday to Friday, 9am to 5pm

You can also use the online self-assessment tool first to check whether an assessment is likely to be the right next step.

### Who can request one

Anyone can request a care needs assessment:

- You can request one for yourself
- A family member, friend, or carer can request one on your behalf
- A GP, hospital, or other professional can refer you

You do not need a referral from a doctor. If you think you might need help, you have the right to request an assessment.

### What happens before the assessment

When we receive your request, we will:

- Confirm we have received it
- Ask you some initial questions to understand your situation
- Arrange a date and time for the assessment that suits you
- Tell you what information it would be helpful to have ready

### What to have ready

It can help to think about the following before your assessment:

- What daily tasks you find difficult (for example, washing, cooking, getting around)
- How your condition or situation affects your daily life
- What support you are already receiving from family, friends, or other services
- What you would like help with
- Any medical conditions, medications, or treatments

You do not need to prepare anything formal. The assessment is a conversation, and the professional will guide you through it.

### Can I bring someone with me?

Yes. You can bring anyone you choose to the assessment — a family member, friend, carer, or anyone you trust. If you do not have anyone to support you and you find it difficult to be involved in the process, we can arrange an independent advocate.

**Callout box:** An assessment does not commit you to anything. You can have an assessment and then decide whether you want to take up any support offered. It is simply a way to understand your situation and your options.

## PAGE 5: WHAT HAPPENS DURING YOUR ASSESSMENT (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How to get support > What happens during your assessment

**H1:** What happens during your assessment

**Standfirst:** Your assessment is a conversation about your daily life. We want to understand what you can do, what you find difficult, and what kind of support would help.

### How the assessment works

Your assessment can take place face to face at your home, over the phone, or by video call. A social care professional from the council will carry out the assessment. If your needs involve health services, an NHS professional may also be involved.

### What we will talk about

The assessment covers your daily life. We will ask about:

- Any physical needs, such as difficulty washing, dressing, preparing meals, or getting around
- Your mental and emotional wellbeing, such as your memory, mood, or how you are coping
- Things that are important to you, such as spending time with family, attending activities, or getting to appointments
- Any support you already receive from family, friends, or other services
- Your home environment and whether it is safe and suitable
- What you would like to achieve and what a good day looks like for you

We will also talk about your strengths — what you can do, not just what you find difficult. We want to understand the full picture so we can recommend the right kind of support.

### How long it takes

A straightforward assessment usually takes around an hour. If your needs are more complex, it may take longer or involve more than one conversation. If your situation is urgent, we will prioritise your assessment.

### What happens afterwards

After your assessment, we will write to you with the outcome. The letter will explain:

- Whether you have eligible care and support needs
- If you are eligible, what happens next — including creating a care and support plan and carrying out a financial assessment to work out whether you need to contribute towards the cost
- If you are not eligible, why that decision was made and what other support is available to you locally

If you disagree with the outcome, you can ask us to reconsider. We will tell you how to do this in the letter.

### If you are not eligible

If your assessment shows that you do not have eligible needs, we will still help. We will give you information and advice about community services, voluntary organisations, equipment, and other support that you can arrange yourself. You can ask for another assessment at any time if your situation changes.

**Callout box:** The assessment is always free. It does not commit you to receiving services or paying anything. It is simply a way to understand your situation and your options.

## PAGE 6: GETTING YOUR CARE AND SUPPORT PLAN (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How to get support > Getting your care and support plan

**H1:** Getting your care and support plan

**Standfirst:** If your assessment shows that you have eligible needs, we will work with you to create a care and support plan. This sets out what help you will receive, how it will be provided, and how it will be paid for.

### What a care and support plan is

A care and support plan is a written document created with you that describes:

- Your eligible care and support needs
- What outcomes you want to achieve — for example, being able to get washed and dressed safely, getting out of the house, or maintaining relationships
- The support that will be provided to meet those needs
- How your support will be arranged — whether by the council, by you through a direct payment, or a combination
- Your personal budget — the amount of money allocated to meet your eligible needs
- Any contribution you need to make towards the cost
- What to do if something goes wrong or your support does not turn up

### Who creates the plan

You are involved in creating your plan. You can write it yourself, work on it with a family member or friend, or do it with support from a social care professional. If you need help and do not have anyone to support you, we can arrange an independent advocate.

The plan must be agreed by both you and the council before your support begins.

### What you can include

Your plan is about you and your life. It can include:

- What is important to you — your routines, priorities, likes and dislikes
- The people who matter to you
- Your hopes and goals
- The kind of support that would make the biggest difference to your daily life
- How you want your support to be arranged

Include things that will genuinely improve your life. Your plan should not contain anything you do not agree with.

### Your personal budget

Everyone whose eligible needs are met by the council receives a personal budget. This is the amount of money needed to meet the cost of your eligible care and support needs. There are different ways your personal budget can be managed:

- **As a direct payment** — the money is paid to you so you can arrange your own support
- **As a council-managed account** — the council arranges support on your behalf
- **As a mix of both** — you manage some of your support and the council manages the rest

For more information, see our page on direct payments and personal budgets.

### How your plan is reviewed

Your care and support plan is reviewed regularly to make sure it is still working for you.

- You will usually have an initial review shortly after your support starts, to check everything is working
- After that, your plan will be reviewed at least once a year
- You can ask for a review at any time if your needs change or your support is not working

During a review, we check whether your needs have changed, whether your support is meeting those needs, and whether your personal budget is still right.

### If you are unhappy

If you disagree with your plan, or if you think your needs are not being met, talk to your social care professional first. If you are still unhappy, you can use the council's complaints process to have your case reviewed independently.
