**H1:** How adult social care works

**Standfirst:** Find out what adult social care is, who it is for, how to get help, and what you may need to pay.

### Section: The journey step by step

If you or someone you know needs help with everyday life because of illness, disability, or getting older, adult social care may be able to help. Here is how the process works:

1. You contact us. You can phone us, use our online self-assessment tool, or ask someone to contact us on your behalf. A GP, hospital, or other professional can also refer you.
2. We assess your needs. A care needs assessment is a conversation about what you can and cannot do, what matters to you, and what support might help. It is always free.
3. We decide if you are eligible. We look at whether your needs arise from a physical or mental condition, whether they affect your ability to do everyday things, and whether this has a significant impact on your wellbeing.
4. We work out the cost. If you are eligible for support, we carry out a financial assessment to decide whether you need to contribute towards the cost of your care.
5. We agree a plan with you. Your care and support plan sets out what help you will receive, how it will be provided, and how it will be paid for.
6. We review your plan regularly. We check that your support is still working for you and make changes if your needs have changed.

**Callout box:** A care needs assessment is always free. Anyone can ask for one. You do not need a referral from a GP or hospital.

### Section: Key things to know

- Adult social care is for people aged 18 and over who need help with everyday life because of illness, disability, frailty, or a mental health condition.
- A care needs assessment is free. You do not need to pay anything to find out whether you are eligible for support.
- Not everyone who has an assessment will receive council-funded care. But even if you are not eligible, we will give you information and advice about other help available to you.
- Adult social care is means-tested. If you are eligible for support, we will assess your finances to see if you need to contribute towards the cost.
- You have choices. If you receive a personal budget, you can choose how your support is arranged, including managing it yourself through a direct payment.

## PAGE 2: WHAT IS ADULT SOCIAL CARE (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How adult social care works > What is adult social care

**H1:** What is adult social care

**Standfirst:** Adult social care is the help and support that some adults need to live as independently and safely as possible.

### What adult social care means

Adult social care covers the support that people aged 18 and over may need because of illness, disability, frailty, or a mental health condition. It can include help at home, support in the community, or care in a residential setting.

The aim is to help you live as independently as possible, to stay safe, and to have a good quality of life. It is not just about the council providing services — it includes the help given by family, friends, carers, and voluntary organisations.

### The kinds of help available

Adult social care can include:

- Help with personal care, such as washing, dressing, and eating
- Support to manage at home, such as cooking, shopping, and housework
- Equipment and adaptations to help you stay safe and independent at home
- Support to get out and about, attend activities, or stay connected to your community
- Short-term support to help you recover after an illness or hospital stay (sometimes called reablement)
- Longer-term care at home or in a care home
- Support for people with learning disabilities, autism, or mental health conditions
- Support and advice for unpaid carers

### Who provides it

In Waltham Forest, adult social care is arranged and funded by the council. Some support is provided directly by the council, and some is provided by other organisations such as care agencies, voluntary groups, and community services.

The council works with NHS services, housing, and other organisations to make sure that the support you receive is joined up.

### Your rights

Under the Care Act 2014, you have the right to:

- Ask for a care needs assessment at any time
- Receive information and advice about the support available, even if you are not eligible for council-funded care
- Be involved in decisions about your care and support
- Have a carer's assessment if you are looking after someone
- Raise concerns about the safety of a vulnerable adult

## PAGE 3: WHO CAN GET HELP (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How adult social care works > Who can get help

**H1:** Who can get help

**Standfirst:** Not everyone who contacts us will receive council-funded care. This page explains what we look at when deciding if you are eligible for support.

### Who adult social care is for

Adult social care is for people aged 18 and over who live in Waltham Forest and have care and support needs arising from a physical or mental impairment or illness. This includes people with physical disabilities, learning disabilities, mental health conditions, sensory impairments, long-term illnesses, and age-related conditions such as dementia or frailty.

### How eligibility works

To decide if you are eligible for council-funded support, we carry out a care needs assessment. The assessment looks at three things:

1. Do your care and support needs arise from a physical or mental impairment or illness?
2. Because of those needs, are you unable to achieve two or more of the things listed below?
3. As a result, is there a significant impact on your wellbeing?

If the answer to all three is yes, you are likely to be eligible for support from the council.

### What we look at

The eligibility criteria look at whether you can do the following things:

- Prepare and eat food and drink
- Wash yourself and look after your personal hygiene
- Use the toilet
- Dress yourself appropriately
- Keep your home safe and clean
- Stay safe from harm, abuse, or neglect
- Maintain relationships with family and friends
- Access work, education, training, or volunteering
- Use local services and facilities
- Carry out any caring responsibilities you have

You do not need to have difficulty with all of these. If you are unable to achieve two or more, and this has a significant impact on your wellbeing, you may be eligible.

### What happens if you are eligible

If your assessment shows that you have eligible needs, we will work with you to create a care and support plan. This sets out what help you will receive and how it will be provided.

We will also carry out a financial assessment to work out whether you need to contribute towards the cost of your care.

### What happens if you are not eligible

If your assessment shows that you do not have eligible needs, we will tell you why. We will also give you information and advice about other support that may help you, including local community services, voluntary organisations, and equipment that you can arrange yourself.

You can ask for another assessment at any time if your situation changes.

**Callout box:** You do not need a GP referral to ask for an assessment. Anyone can contact us — you, a family member, a friend, or a professional who works with you.

## PAGE 4: HOW TO ASK FOR HELP (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How adult social care works > How to ask for help

**H1:** How to ask for help

**Standfirst:** If you think you or someone you know might need adult social care, you can contact us to ask for an assessment. You do not need a referral from a GP or hospital.

**Button:** Use our self-assessment tool
(Links to the existing self-assessment tool on the portal)

### Who can ask

Anyone can contact us to ask for a care needs assessment:

- You can ask for yourself
- A family member, friend, or carer can ask on your behalf
- A GP, nurse, hospital, or other health professional can refer you
- Another organisation, such as a housing provider or voluntary group, can contact us about you

You do not need to be in crisis to ask for help. If you are finding everyday tasks more difficult than they used to be, it is worth getting in touch.

### How to contact us

**Phone:** 020 8496 3000
Monday to Friday, 9am to 5pm

**Online:** Use our self-assessment tool to answer some questions about your situation. The tool will tell you whether you might be eligible for support and direct you to information and services that could help.

**In an emergency:** If you or someone you know is in immediate danger, call 999. If you need urgent social care help outside office hours, call 020 8496 3000 and choose the emergency option.

### What happens when you contact us

When you get in touch, we will:

- Listen to your situation and understand what kind of help you are looking for
- Give you information and advice about the support available
- Decide whether a care needs assessment is the right next step
- If an assessment is needed, arrange a time that suits you

In some cases, we may be able to help straight away by putting you in touch with community services, equipment providers, or other support without the need for a formal assessment.

### If you are a professional

If you are a GP, nurse, social worker, or other professional and you want to refer someone for adult social care support, please contact us by phone on 020 8496 3000.

**Callout box:** The sooner you contact us, the sooner we can help. Many people wait until they are in crisis before asking for support. Getting in touch early means we can connect you to help that may prevent things from getting worse.

## PAGE 5: WHAT HAPPENS DURING AN ASSESSMENT (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How adult social care works > What happens during an assessment

**H1:** What happens during an assessment

**Standfirst:** A care needs assessment is a conversation about your daily life, what you find difficult, and what support might help. It is not a test.

### What an assessment is

A care needs assessment is a discussion between you and a professional from the council. Its purpose is to understand your needs, your strengths, and what matters to you. The goal is to work out the best way to help you stay as independent as possible.

An assessment is not a test. There are no right or wrong answers. It is about understanding your situation so we can help.

### How it works

Your assessment can take place face to face, over the phone, or by video call. It will usually involve talking about:

- Any physical needs you have, such as difficulty washing, dressing, preparing meals, or getting around
- Your mental and emotional wellbeing, such as your memory, mood, or how you are coping
- The things that are important to you, such as spending time with family, attending activities, or getting to appointments
- Any support you are already receiving from family, friends, or other services
- Your home environment and whether it is safe and suitable for your needs
- What you would like to achieve and what a good day looks like for you

### Who will be there

A social care professional from the council will carry out the assessment. If your needs involve health services, an NHS professional may also be involved.

You can bring someone with you to the assessment — a family member, friend, or anyone you trust. If you do not have anyone to support you and you find it difficult to be involved in the process, we can arrange an independent advocate to help.

### What happens afterwards

After your assessment, we will write to you with the outcome. This will explain:

- Whether you have eligible care and support needs
- If you are eligible, what happens next — including creating a care and support plan and carrying out a financial assessment
- If you are not eligible, why that decision was made and what other support is available to you

If you disagree with the outcome, you can ask for it to be reconsidered. We will tell you how to do this in the letter we send you.

**Callout box:** A care needs assessment is always free. It does not commit you to anything. You can have an assessment and then decide whether you want to take up any support offered.

### How long does it take?

The length of an assessment depends on your situation. A straightforward assessment may take around an hour. If your needs are more complex, it may take longer or involve more than one conversation.

We aim to complete assessments as quickly as possible. If your situation is urgent, we will prioritise your assessment.

## PAGE 6: YOUR CARE AND SUPPORT PLAN (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > How adult social care works > Your care and support plan

**H1:** Your care and support plan

**Standfirst:** If you are eligible for support, we will work with you to create a care and support plan. This sets out what help you will receive, how it will be provided, and how it will be paid for.

### What a care and support plan is

A care and support plan is a written document that describes your needs, what outcomes you want to achieve, and how your support will be arranged. It is based on what was discussed during your assessment.

The plan should reflect who you are — your lifestyle, your preferences, your strengths, and what matters to you. Anyone reading it should understand what kind of support you need and why.

### What your plan includes

Your care and support plan will cover:

- Your eligible care and support needs
- The outcomes you want to achieve — for example, being able to get washed and dressed safely, getting out of the house, or maintaining relationships
- The support that will be provided to meet those needs
- How your support will be arranged — whether by the council, by you through a direct payment, or a combination
- Your personal budget — the amount of money allocated to meet your eligible needs
- Any contribution you need to make towards the cost
- What to do if something goes wrong or your support does not turn up

### Who creates the plan

You are involved in creating your plan. You can do it yourself, with the help of a family member or friend, or with support from a social care professional. If you need help and do not have anyone to support you, we can arrange an independent advocate.

The plan must be agreed by both you and the council before your support begins.

### What you can include

Your plan is about you. It can include:

- What is important to you — your likes, dislikes, routines, and priorities
- The people who matter to you
- Your hopes for the future
- The kind of support that would make the biggest difference to your daily life

Put things in your plan that will genuinely improve your life. Your plan does not need to include anything you do not agree with.

### How your plan is reviewed

Your care and support plan will be reviewed regularly to make sure it is still meeting your needs. You will usually have an initial review shortly after your support starts, to check that everything is working as expected. After that, your plan will be reviewed at least once a year.

You can ask for a review at any time if your needs change, if your support is not working, or if you want to change how your budget is managed.

During a review, we will check:

- Whether your support is meeting your needs
- Whether you are achieving the outcomes in your plan
- Whether your needs have changed
- Whether your personal budget is still right for your situation

### If you are unhappy

If you disagree with your plan, or if you think your needs are not being met, talk to your social care professional. If you are still unhappy, you can use the council's complaints process to have your case reviewed by an independent person.
