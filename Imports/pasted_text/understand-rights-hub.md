## PAGE 1: UNDERSTAND YOUR RIGHTS (Hub landing page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Understand your rights

**H1:** Understand your rights

**Standfirst:** The Care Act 2014 gives you clear rights when it comes to adult social care. This section explains what you are entitled to, how to get independent support, and what to do if things go wrong.

### Section: Your key rights

Under the law, you have the right to:

- **Ask for a care needs assessment** at any time. The council must assess anyone who appears to need care and support. The assessment is always free.
- **Be involved in decisions about your care.** The council must work with you to plan your care and support. Your views, wishes, and goals must be taken into account.
- **Receive information and advice** about the care and support available to you, even if you are not eligible for council-funded services.
- **Have choice and control** over how your care is arranged. If you receive a personal budget, you can choose to manage it yourself through a direct payment.
- **Have a carer's assessment** if you are an unpaid carer. You have the same right to an assessment and support as the person you care for.
- **Access an independent advocate** if you have difficulty being involved in decisions about your care and you do not have anyone appropriate to support you.
- **Be protected from abuse and neglect.** The council has a duty to safeguard adults who have care and support needs and are at risk of harm.
- **Complain** if something goes wrong. You have a legal right to raise a complaint about any aspect of your adult social care, and it will not affect the care you receive.
- **Challenge a decision.** If you disagree with a decision about your care or your financial assessment, you can ask for it to be reviewed.

## PAGE 2: YOUR RIGHTS UNDER THE CARE ACT (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Understand your rights > Your rights under the Care Act

**H1:** Your rights under the Care Act

**Standfirst:** The Care Act 2014 sets out what councils must do when it comes to adult social care. Here is what it means for you, in plain English.

### The right to an assessment

Anyone who appears to need care and support can ask for a care needs assessment. The council cannot refuse. You do not need a GP referral, and the assessment is always free. It does not matter whether the council thinks you are likely to be eligible — if you ask, they must assess you.

### The right to be involved

The council must involve you in decisions about your care. This means your assessment, your care plan, and any review of your care should be done with you, not to you. Your views, your wishes, and the outcomes that matter to you must be taken into account.

If you find it difficult to be involved — for example, because of a learning disability, dementia, or a mental health condition — and you do not have a family member or friend who can help, the council must arrange an independent advocate for you.

### The right to information and advice

The council must provide information and advice about the care and support available in the area. This applies to everyone — not just people who are eligible for council-funded care. If you are a self-funder, you still have the right to information, advice, and help finding suitable care.

### The right to choice and control

If you are eligible for council-funded care, you will receive a personal budget. You can choose how this is managed — including receiving it as a direct payment so you can arrange your own support. The council must offer you this choice.

### The right to continuity of care

If you move from one council area to another, the Care Act says you must continue to receive your care and support on the day you arrive in the new area. The two councils must work together to make sure there is no gap in your care.

### The right to safeguarding

The council has a duty to protect adults who have care and support needs and are at risk of abuse or neglect. If you are being harmed, or you are worried about someone else, you have the right to raise a concern and have it investigated.

### Services that must be free

Under the Care Act, the following must always be provided free of charge:

- Care needs assessments and care planning
- Intermediate care and reablement services (for up to six weeks)
- Community equipment and minor adaptations costing £1,000 or less
- After-care services under Section 117 of the Mental Health Act 1983
- Information, advice, and guidance

### Where to find the Care Act

The full text of the Care Act 2014 is available on legislation.gov.uk. The government has also published detailed statutory guidance explaining how councils should apply the Act.

## PAGE 3: INDEPENDENT ADVOCACY (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Understand your rights > Independent advocacy

**H1:** Independent advocacy

**Standfirst:** An advocate is someone who helps you express your views, understand your rights, and make sure your voice is heard when decisions are being made about your care.

### What an advocate does

An independent advocate is not a council employee. They work for a separate organisation and are there to represent your interests. An advocate can:

- Help you understand what is happening and what your options are
- Support you to express your views in meetings, assessments, or reviews
- Make sure the council listens to what you want
- Help you challenge a decision if you think it is wrong
- Support you through the complaints process

An advocate does not make decisions for you. They help you make your own decisions, or make sure your wishes are represented if you are unable to do this yourself.

### When the council must arrange an advocate

Under the Care Act 2014, the council has a legal duty to arrange an independent advocate for you if two conditions are met:

1. You have difficulty being involved in decisions about your care — for example, because of a learning disability, dementia, a mental health condition, or a communication difficulty.
2. There is no one appropriate available to support you — for example, you do not have a family member or friend who is able and willing to help.

This applies to care needs assessments, care and support planning, care reviews, and safeguarding enquiries. The council must consider whether you need an advocate at each of these stages.

### Types of advocacy

There are different types of independent advocacy, depending on your situation:

- **Care Act advocacy** — for people who need support to be involved in their care and support under the Care Act 2014
- **Independent Mental Health Advocacy (IMHA)** — for people who are detained under the Mental Health Act, or who are subject to a Community Treatment Order or guardianship
- **Independent Mental Capacity Advocacy (IMCA)** — for people who lack mental capacity and have no one else to represent them when serious decisions are being made about their care or treatment
- **General advocacy** — broader support to help you navigate services, raise concerns, or understand your rights

### How to get an advocate

You can ask the council to arrange an advocate for you. Your social worker, care coordinator, or anyone involved in your care can also make a referral.

You can also contact the advocacy service directly:

**POhWER** provides independent advocacy services in Waltham Forest for adults.

Phone: 0300 456 2370
Website: pohwer.net/waltham-forest

**Callout box:** You do not need to wait for the council to offer you an advocate. If you think you need one, ask. If you find it difficult to be involved in decisions about your care and you do not have someone to help you, the council must arrange an advocate — this is a legal duty, not a discretionary service.

## PAGE 4: MENTAL CAPACITY AND DECISION-MAKING (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Understand your rights > Mental capacity and decision-making

**H1:** Mental capacity and decision-making

**Standfirst:** You have the right to make your own decisions. If there are concerns about whether you are able to make a specific decision, the law sets out clear rules to protect you.

### The right to make your own decisions

The Mental Capacity Act 2005 starts from the principle that every adult has the right to make their own decisions. You must be assumed to have capacity unless it is established otherwise. Making a decision that others disagree with does not mean you lack capacity.

### The five principles

The Mental Capacity Act is built on five core principles:

1. **Assume capacity.** Every adult is assumed to be able to make their own decisions unless it is proved otherwise.
2. **Support decision-making.** Before deciding that someone lacks capacity, all practical steps must be taken to help them make the decision themselves.
3. **Unwise decisions are allowed.** A person is not to be treated as lacking capacity just because they make a decision that others consider unwise.
4. **Best interests.** If a decision must be made on behalf of someone who lacks capacity, it must be made in their best interests.
5. **Least restrictive option.** Any action taken on behalf of someone who lacks capacity should be the least restrictive of their rights and freedom.

### What mental capacity means

Mental capacity means the ability to make a specific decision at a specific time. A person lacks capacity if, because of an illness or disability affecting how their mind works, they are unable to:

- Understand the information relevant to the decision
- Retain that information long enough to make the decision
- Use or weigh the information to make the decision
- Communicate their decision (by any means)

Capacity is assessed on a decision-by-decision basis. Someone may have capacity to make some decisions but not others. Capacity can also change over time.

### Best interests decisions

If someone is assessed as lacking capacity to make a particular decision, that decision must be made in their best interests. This means considering:

- The person's past and present wishes, feelings, beliefs, and values
- The views of anyone involved in their care, including family members and carers
- Whether the person is likely to regain capacity and whether the decision can wait
- The least restrictive option available

### Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards (DoLS)

If someone who lacks capacity is living in a care home or hospital and their care arrangements amount to a deprivation of their liberty, the care home or hospital must apply for authorisation under the Deprivation of Liberty Safeguards. This is a legal protection to make sure that any restrictions on a person's freedom are lawful, necessary, and in their best interests.

### Lasting Power of Attorney

A Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA) allows you to appoint someone you trust to make decisions on your behalf if you lose the capacity to make them yourself. There are two types:

- **Property and financial affairs LPA** — covers decisions about your money, property, and financial matters
- **Health and welfare LPA** — covers decisions about your medical treatment and care, including where you live

You can only make an LPA while you have the mental capacity to do so. For more information, visit the Office of the Public Guardian on GOV.UK.

### Court of Protection

If someone lacks capacity and does not have a Lasting Power of Attorney, the Court of Protection can appoint a deputy to make decisions on their behalf. The court can also make one-off decisions about someone's welfare, property, or finances.

## PAGE 5: YOUR RIGHTS AS A CARER (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Understand your rights > Your rights as a carer

**H1:** Your rights as a carer

**Standfirst:** If you provide unpaid care for a family member, friend, or neighbour, the law gives you specific rights. You are entitled to support in your own right — not just as an extension of the person you care for.

### What the Care Act says about carers

Since April 2015, the Care Act 2014 has given unpaid carers the same rights to assessment and support as the people they care for. This means the council must treat your needs as equally important.

### Your right to a carer's assessment

If you provide regular and substantial care for another person, you have the right to ask for a carer's assessment. This is a conversation about how caring affects your life, what you find difficult, and what support would help.

You are entitled to an assessment regardless of the level of care you provide, the financial resources of either you or the person you care for, or whether the person you look after has been assessed by the council.

To request a carer's assessment, contact the Adult Social Care team on 020 8496 3130, or ask Carers First (who provide carer support services on behalf of the council).

### What the assessment looks at

A carer's assessment considers whether your caring role is affecting your ability to:

- Carry out day-to-day tasks at home
- Look after your own physical and mental health
- Maintain relationships with family and friends
- Participate in work, education, or training
- Have time for leisure and social activities
- Achieve the things that matter to you

If your caring role is having a significant impact on your wellbeing in any of these areas, you may be eligible for support.

### Support you may be entitled to

If your assessment shows you have eligible needs, the council must provide support. This might include:

- Replacement care (sometimes called respite) so you can take a break
- Help with tasks around the home
- A personal budget or direct payment to arrange your own support
- Referral to community services, peer support groups, or counselling
- Information about benefits you may be entitled to, including Carer's Allowance

Support for carers is provided free of charge. The council does not charge for services provided directly to you as a carer in your own right.

### Other protections for carers

In addition to the Care Act, other laws protect your rights as a carer:

- **Equality Act 2010** — protects you from discrimination related to your caring role
- **Employment Rights Act 1996** — gives you the right to take time off for emergencies involving a dependant
- **Flexible working regulations** — give you the right to request flexible working arrangements if you are an employee

### Carers First

Carers First provides support services for unpaid carers on behalf of the council. They offer practical and emotional support, advice, information, peer support groups, and training.

Website: carersfirst.org.uk/waltham-forest

### Further information

- Carers UK — information about carers' rights, benefits, and support (carersuk.org)
- GOV.UK — Carer's Allowance information and applications

## PAGE 6: CHALLENGING DECISIONS AND RAISING CONCERNS (Sub-page)

**Breadcrumb:** Home > Adult social care > Understand your rights > Challenging decisions and raising concerns

**H1:** Challenging decisions and raising concerns

**Standfirst:** If you disagree with a decision about your care, your financial assessment, or the way you have been treated, you have the right to challenge it. Here is how.

### Talk to your social worker or care coordinator first

If you are unhappy with a decision, the quickest way to resolve it is usually to speak directly to the person involved — your social worker, care coordinator, or the team that made the decision. Many issues can be sorted out through a conversation without needing a formal process.

### Challenging a care needs assessment decision

If you disagree with the outcome of your care needs assessment — for example, if you think you should have been found eligible for support, or if your assessed needs do not reflect your situation — you can:

- Ask the council to explain the decision in writing, including the reasons
- Provide additional information that was not available during the assessment
- Ask for a reassessment if your circumstances have changed
- Make a formal complaint if you believe the assessment was carried out incorrectly

### Challenging a financial assessment

If you think your financial assessment is wrong, you have the right to ask for it to be reviewed. The council's Fairer Contributions Policy sets out a clear appeals process:

1. **Stage 1 — Review** by the Financial Assessment Unit. You can ask for the calculation to be checked and any errors corrected. The team must respond within 10 working days.
2. **Stage 2 — First Appeal** to the Head of Service. You can submit additional evidence. The Head of Service must respond within 10 working days.
3. **Stage 3 — Second Appeal** to the Corporate Director of Adult Social Care. The Corporate Director has the authority to temporarily waive contributions in exceptional circumstances. Response within 10 working days.

### Challenging a care and support plan

If you disagree with the content of your care and support plan — for example, the level of support offered, the way it is being provided, or the personal budget amount — you can:

- Discuss it with your social worker and ask for the plan to be reconsidered
- Request a review of your plan at any time
- Ask for an independent advocate to help you put your views across
- Make a formal complaint

### Making a formal complaint

If you have tried to resolve the issue informally and are not satisfied, you can make a formal complaint about any aspect of your adult social care. The council must acknowledge your complaint within 3 working days and aim to respond within 20 working days.

Making a complaint will not affect the care you receive. You have a legal right to complain.

See our complaints page for how to submit a complaint.

### The Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman

If you have been through the council's complaints process and are still not satisfied, you can take your complaint to the Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman (LGSCO). The Ombudsman is independent and can investigate complaints about adult social care, including failures to act, delays, poor decisions, and unfair treatment.

Website: lgo.org.uk

### Judicial review

In rare cases, if you believe the council has acted unlawfully — for example, by failing to carry out a legal duty or making a decision that is irrational — you may be able to seek a judicial review through the courts. You should get legal advice before taking this step. Citizens Advice or a solicitor specialising in community care law can help.

### Getting independent help

If you need support to challenge a decision or raise a concern:

- **POhWER** — independent advocacy (pohwer.net/waltham-forest, phone 0300 456 2370)
- **Healthwatch Waltham Forest** — independent voice for health and social care users (healthwatchwalthamforest.co.uk)
- **Citizens Advice Waltham Forest** — free, confidential advice on your rights
- **Local Government and Social Care Ombudsman** — independent complaints investigation (lgo.org.uk)

**Callout box:** You should never feel afraid to challenge a decision about your care. The law gives you clear rights to be heard, to have decisions explained, and to have them reconsidered if they are wrong. Raising a concern or making a complaint will not affect the care you receive.
