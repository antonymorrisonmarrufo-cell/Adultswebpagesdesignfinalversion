SECTION: What is occupational therapy?
Copy:
Occupational therapy helps people manage everyday tasks more safely and independently when age, illness, disability or a long-term health condition makes those tasks harder.

An occupational therapist looks at how you manage daily activities in your home and recommends practical solutions. This might include advice, equipment, minor adaptations or, where appropriate, major changes to your home.

SECTION: Who can get help?
Copy:
You can ask for help if all of the following apply:
- you are aged 18 or over
- you live permanently in Waltham Forest
- you have a permanent and substantial disability or long-term health condition
- you find it difficult to carry out essential daily activities safely on your own

A family member, friend or carer can contact us on your behalf if you consent.

If you are under 18, use the children’s occupational therapy route instead of this adult service.

SECTION: What can we help with?
Copy:
We can help with difficulties such as:
- getting in and out of bed
- getting on and off the toilet
- washing, bathing and dressing
- using stairs safely
- getting in and out of your home
- moving around your home
- preparing simple meals
- using equipment or simple adaptations to stay independent

We may recommend:
- advice on safer ways to do everyday tasks
- equipment and minor adaptations
- major adaptations to your home where appropriate
- signposting to related community support

SECTION: Home adaptations
Copy:
For many people, adapting their current home is the best way to stay independent.

Minor adaptations might include:
- grab rails
- stair rails
- portable ramps
- lever taps
- raised toilet seats
- bath boards
- small threshold changes

Major adaptations might include:
- widening doorways for wheelchair access
- permanent ramps
- replacing a bath with a level-access shower
- adding a downstairs toilet, bathroom or bedroom
- changing the layout of a kitchen

If your home cannot be adapted safely or reasonably, moving to a more suitable property may sometimes be a better option.

- wheelchairs, mobility scooters or walking sticks

SECTION: What happens next
Copy:
After you make a request:
1. We will acknowledge your request.
2. A member of our team will review the information and decide whether an occupational therapy assessment is needed.
3. If needed, we will arrange a home or virtual assessment.
4. We will agree recommendations with you. This may include advice, equipment, minor adaptations, or where appropriate a referral for major adaptations.

If you choose to buy equipment yourself or carry out work privately, you are responsible for the cost.

- improving balance and leg strength
- removing clutter and trip hazards
- improving lighting
- reviewing unsuitable footwear
- using the right equipment
- getting advice if you feel unsteady at home

SECTION: What telecare and community alarm can do
Copy:
Telecare and community alarm services can help people live more safely and independently at home.

They can provide:
- a way to call for help quickly in an emergency
- reassurance if you live alone
- extra peace of mind for family and carers
- sensors that detect risks and raise an alert if something is wrong

SECTION: Community alarm only
Copy:
A community alarm is a telephone unit linked to a pendant or wristband.

When you press the red button, it connects 24 hours a day, 365 days a year to a control centre.

The control centre can:
- speak to you
- contact relatives, friends or neighbours
- contact the emergency services if needed

To qualify for a community alarm, you may need to meet one or more of these criteria:
- live alone
- be housebound
- be home alone regularly while relatives or carers are out
- feel vulnerable at home
- be a carer of a disabled child or adult who may need emergency help
- need a way to contact a doctor or someone else in an emergency

SECTION: Community alarm with telecare sensors
Copy:
Telecare adds sensors around the home to help detect problems and raise an alert if needed.

For example, sensors may detect:
- if someone leaves the house at an unsafe time
- if someone does not return to bed after getting up in the night
- smoke, gas or heat
- movement that may alert a carer in the same home

If an alarm is raised, the operator will check the person is safe and may contact a relative, mobile warden or emergency services.

SECTION: Who can get it
Copy:
Community alarm and telecare may be suitable if you:
- live alone
- feel vulnerable at home
- need extra reassurance because of a health condition, disability or memory problem
- need support to remain safe at home
- are worried about someone who may need help in an emergency

H1:
Callers to your door

Standfirst:
Stay safe from doorstep callers by checking ID, using a door chain, avoiding pressure, and never sharing personal details.

SECTION: Why this matters
Copy:
Many legitimate companies call at people’s homes, but criminals also use doorstep visits to pressure people into sharing information or paying for things they do not want.

They may pretend to be:
- utility workers
- officials
- survey staff
- traders
- people checking something at your home

SECTION: Ways to protect yourself
Copy:
Ways to protect yourself include:
- fit a door chain and spy hole if possible
- always ask for and check identification
- do not let people in if you are unsure who they are
- if you are interested in something, ask them to leave contact details so you can arrange a later appointment
- do not sign on the spot
- get at least three written quotes for work
- never share personal or financial details at the door

SECTION: If you decide to buy
Copy:
If you do decide to buy something:
- ask for references from previous customers
- get any agreement in writing
- check the cooling-off period
- do not pay for work before it is completed and you are satisfied

Fraud over the phone

Standfirst:
Stay safe from phone scams by pausing, checking who is calling, and never sharing money or personal information under pressure.

SECTION: How phone fraud works
Copy:
Fraudsters often call pretending to be from a trusted organisation, such as:
- your bank
- the police
- a utility company
- a government service
- a delivery company

SECTION: How to protect yourself
Copy:

- do not use a phone number given by the caller
- never transfer money because of an unexpected phone call
- talk to someone you trust if you are unsure
