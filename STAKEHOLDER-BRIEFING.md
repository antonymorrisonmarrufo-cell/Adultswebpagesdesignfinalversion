# Stakeholder Briefing: Adult Social Care Website Redesign

**Prepared for:** Senior Leadership, Adult Social Care
**Date:** March 2026
**Status:** Draft design complete — awaiting stakeholder review and sign-off

---

## Executive Summary

We have completed a full redesign of Waltham Forest Council's Adult Social Care web pages. The new design comprises **152 pages across 17 sections** (219 source files), rebuilt from the ground up to deliver three strategic outcomes:

1. **Early intervention and prevention** — directing residents to community support before they reach formal services
2. **Promoting independence** — helping people stay in their own homes longer
3. **Reducing Care Act assessment routing** — ensuring only appropriate cases reach the assessment pathway

The prototype is live and ready for stakeholder review.

---

## Why We Did This

### The Problem

Research and call analysis of the Adults Front Door revealed:

- **No strengths-based conversations** — front-door agents were filling out screening forms and routing directly to assessment without exploring what people could do for themselves
- **No signposting to community resources** — not a single first-time assessment call involved directing the caller to voluntary or community services
- **High volume of unnecessary assessments** — residents were entering the formal Care Act assessment process when community support, equipment, or information and advice would have met their needs
- **Poor digital self-service** — the existing website was difficult to navigate, content was dense and scattered, and there was no clear pathway for residents to help themselves online

### The Opportunity

A well-designed digital front door can:
- **Deflect** a significant proportion of calls and assessment referrals to self-service and community support
- **Inform** residents about what's available without needing to call
- **Empower** people to maintain independence through equipment, telecare, social prescribing, and local groups
- **Support carers early** before they reach crisis point
- Evidence from other councils shows that prevention-first approaches and digital self-service can reduce assessment demand by 15–25%

## What We Built

### Design Principles

Every design decision was guided by these principles:

| Principle | What it means in practice |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Prevention first** | Community support, self-help, and voluntary sector options appear before formal council services on every page |
| **Independence by default** | Content focuses on what people CAN do, not what they can't. Equipment, adaptations, and self-management come before care packages |
| **Reduce assessment demand** | "Check your situation online" is the primary call-to-action, not the phone number. "You may not need a formal assessment" messaging is prominent |
| **Strengths-based language** | Empowering, person-centred language throughout — not deficit-based or bureaucratic |
| **Plain English** | Written for residents, not professionals. No jargon, clear headings, scannable content |
| **Task-oriented navigation** | Organised by what people need to do ("How to ask for help", "Check your situation") not by council structure |
| **Accessibility** | WCAG-aligned, clear visual hierarchy, high contrast, mobile-responsive |
| **GOV.UK alignment** | Consistent with UK government web design standards |

The site uses four standardised box types across all pages:

| Box Type | Background | Border | Heading Colour | Icon |
|----------|------------|--------|----------------|------|
| **Navigation cards** | White | None | Digital Rose `#bf3688` | Pink circle arrow icon |
| **Contact/info boxes** | Light blue `#d7f8ff` | Full Navy `#003078` border | Navy `#003078` | Info icon |
| **Info/bullet list boxes** | White | None | Inherits page style | None |
| **Emergency/warning boxes** | Light red `#fcedf3` | Full Red `#d4351c` border | Red `#d4351c` | None |

These four patterns cover every content block in the site and ensure visual consistency across all 152 pages.

## Key Design Decisions and Reasoning

### 1. "You might not need a formal assessment" — Landing Page

**What we did:** The main Adult Social Care landing page now leads with a green callout stating "You might not need a formal assessment" with a prominent "Check your situation online" button. The phone number is demoted to a smaller secondary box below.

**Why:** The old design's first action was "call us" — which routes directly to the front door team and into assessment triage. The new design encourages self-service first, reducing call volume and unnecessary assessments.

### 2. Community Support Step in the Support Journey

**What we did:** "How to get support" now includes "Explore community and local support" as Step 2, before "Contact us" or "Request an assessment". Links to social prescribing, voluntary sector, and local groups.

**Why:** The call analysis showed zero signposting to community resources. This step ensures every digital user sees community options before reaching the formal pathway.

### 3. Live Independently Section — Prevention Cards First

**What we did:** Reordered the section so falls prevention, social prescribing, equipment, and telecare appear first. Added a callout: "You do not need a formal assessment to access many of these services."

**Why:** These services prevent escalation. A grab rail installed today prevents a fall and hospital admission tomorrow. Making these prominent encourages self-referral and reduces formal assessment demand.

### 4. Carers Universal Offer Made Visible

**What we did:** The Being a Carer section now leads with the Carers Hub and a new "Universal Offer" card — support available to all carers without any assessment. The carers assessment page is moved from 1st to 4th position.

**Why:** Many carers, especially new or informal carers, need peer support, information, and a break — not a formal assessment. Making the universal offer visible catches them early before they reach crisis.

### 5. Contact Page — Self-Service Before Phone

**What we did:** The Contact Us page now starts with "Before you call, check online" and shows community support options before listing the phone number.

**Why:** Every call answered is a cost. Every call that could have been resolved by self-service is a missed prevention opportunity. This reordering should measurably reduce call volume.

### 6. Dementia — Community Before Clinical

**What we did:** Activities and social groups now appear above the memory assessment service. The help section leads with community support before GP referral. Contact details include the Dementia Hub and adviser service before the ASC team number.

**Why:** For people already diagnosed, community activities and the adviser service are more relevant than the memory clinic. For carers, practical help matters more than assessment.
